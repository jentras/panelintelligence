How AI is Reshaping Industries

Moderator K Kerin VanAndel opened with a deceptively simple prompt: how is AI reshaping your field right now? What followed was a snapshot of an economy mid-transformation — from operating rooms to construction sites.

"It's disrupting — I think cerebrally, mentally disrupting everyone. Everyone's up at night thinking about how it's going to affect us personally."
P Pritha Sridharan · OC General Manager, Slalom

Pritha offered a consultant's vantage point — seeing dozens of clients across every sector at different stages of AI adoption. At Slalom, the integration is end-to-end: from how proposals are written to how consultants research niche subindustries. AI isn't disrupting consulting — it's re-energizing it.

T Thomas Andriola gave the panel's most data-rich examples. In healthcare, ambient listening technology now captures patient-doctor conversations in real time, auto-generating clinical notes and reimbursement documentation. The physician gets back something they have wanted for decades: the ability to focus on the patient.

"It gives the doctor back what they've wanted most — to focus on the patient, not on documentation."
T Thomas Andriola · Vice Chancellor IT & CDO, UC Irvine

The Upskilling Challenge

When VanAndel pivoted to upskilling, A Andrew Gonzales gave the panel its most grounding moment. Representing the LA/OC Building and Construction Trades Council, he noted that skilled trades have been continuously upskilling for decades — technology changes, tools change, processes change. That part isn't new.

What's new is the emotional dimension: not everyone wants to change the nature of their work, and that desire deserves respect.

"Tell it to the bricklayer and the welder. They're going to want to do that work. And welding is a very good paying job — probably pays more than many here in this room."
A Andrew Gonzales · Council Rep, LA/OC Building & Construction Trades

Andriola built on this with UCI's approach: make it an "all of us" topic. The university created AI academies, gave access to tools broadly, and ran hackathons for teams to innovate on their own productivity. The goal was not compliance but agency — people who want to learn get the tools; they choose how far to push.

"We're moving from 'AI won't take your job, someone who knows AI will' — to 'the person who can redefine the work using AI is the most valuable employee.'"
T Thomas Andriola · Vice Chancellor IT & CDO, UC Irvine
Ethical Integration

VanAndel steered the panel toward ethics: how do you use AI without eroding human judgment? Sridharan drew the line at authenticity. When AI writes college essays or builds resumes, it may technically help — but it undermines the signal those documents are meant to send. The ethical question isn't capability, it's displacement of genuine expression.

Andriola framed ethics through governance: UCI has a published AI ethics policy, is a member of the AAU AI Ethics consortium, and is actively developing generative AI guidelines that distinguish use from misuse. The analogy offered was calculators — at some point, society decided calculators in classrooms were acceptable. AI is that same conversation, happening faster and at greater scale.

"At some point you need to decide, as a societal level, what's the equivalent of calculators in the classroom for generative AI."
T Thomas Andriola · Vice Chancellor IT & CDO, UC Irvine
Human Touch & Labor

Gonzales was the panel's honest skeptic — not anti-technology, but clear-eyed about who bears the cost of disruption. When jobs are displaced, the narrative of "new jobs will be created elsewhere" is cold comfort to the family that just lost its income today.

"Yeah, we're gonna create a job someplace else. But tell that to the family that just lost their income."
A Andrew Gonzales · Council Rep, LA/OC Building & Construction Trades

The registered apprenticeship model he champions is itself a proof of concept: pay workers while they train, avoid crushing student debt, and build loyalty to the trade. That model — learning embedded in doing, earning while growing — may be exactly the blueprint the AI transition needs.

Sridharan echoed this with the workplace strategy lens: JLL's work on physical environments mirrors the organizational challenge. The office is not just about where you sit — it's a signal of how you think about people. The same applies to AI rollouts.

The Future of AI Roles

The panel's closing minutes turned to what's next. Andriola is betting on AI agents — not just AI tools. The progression he outlined: first, AI assists with tasks. Then, AI agents complete tasks autonomously. Finally, humans manage portfolios of AI agents. The new skill isn't knowing how to do the task; it's knowing how to direct, evaluate, and trust the agent doing it.

"Be intentional about how you're spending your human time. Where does the human touch matter most — and protect that space."
A Andrew Gonzales · Council Rep, LA/OC Building & Construction Trades

For Sridharan, the advice was practical: start with your own workflow. Find something AI can do better or faster. Get comfortable. Then scale. The organizations winning with AI aren't the ones with the biggest budgets — they're the ones who created cultures of psychological safety to experiment.

Key Takeaways
01

AI is reducing administrative burden, not creative/care work — yet

The biggest early wins (ambient clinical notes, discharge automation, document drafting) are all about removing low-value paperwork. The work that requires judgment, empathy, and presence is still firmly human territory.

↳ Thomas Andriola, Pritha Sridharan
02

The most honest thing said: not everyone wants to change their work

Andrew Gonzales cut through the upskilling hype. A welder who loves welding and earns $90K doesn't need to become a prompt engineer. Respecting that is part of ethical AI integration.

↳ Andrew Gonzales
03

The progression: tool → agent → portfolio manager

UCI's CDO laid out the arc clearly. First you use AI as a tool. Then you deploy AI agents autonomously. The final form of AI-era work is managing a portfolio of agents — a fundamentally different skill set.

↳ Thomas Andriola
04

"All of us" mandates beat "some of us" pilots

UCI gave every employee, student, and professor access to AI tools simultaneously. The signal sent: this isn't for the tech team — it's an institution-wide capability shift. Organizations treating AI as a departmental experiment are behind.

↳ Thomas Andriola
05

Displacement cost falls on individuals, gains accrue to organizations

The panel's sharpest tension: productivity gains from AI are captured by companies and institutions. The disruption costs — retraining, job loss, identity change — are borne by workers. This asymmetry is the ethical fault line.

↳ Andrew Gonzales
06

The "calculator moment" framing is the most useful policy heuristic

Society eventually decided calculators in math class were fine. That decision required debate, norms, and time. AI in knowledge work, education, and hiring needs the same deliberate societal negotiation — and it's already underway.

↳ Thomas Andriola

Government as Investor & Customer

Jeff Burton opened with a reframe: forget the TV narrative of DOGE cuts and agency chaos. Even in the current environment, there are over 1,600 open grant opportunities at the federal level totaling $80 billion, targeting 80,000 recipients. Add another $23 billion at the state level. The opportunity is not shrinking — it's shifting.

John Keisler built on this with the LP metaphor: the federal government put $6.75 trillion into the economy in 2024. California's state budget added $300 billion more. When an anchor investor of that scale commits, private capital follows. That's not ideology — it's why the US attracts over 50% of global venture capital, and California captures more than half of that.

"The economy itself is a partnership between government and the private sector. The reason we have a $24 trillion economy is because we have one of the greatest models in the world — where government is the biggest LP."
J John Keisler · CEO, Sunstone

The reframe goes further: government is not just an investor — it's the nation's largest customer. No entrepreneur has captured more government subsidies, contracts (NASA, DoD), and infrastructure than the current wealthiest person on earth. That's not an accident; it's a playbook.

What the Government Is Actually Buying

Keisler came from Milken Global the week prior, where Commerce Secretary Lutnick repeated one word three times: abundant. The administration's core mandate is growth — stimulating the economy, catalyzing private investment, and above all, energy. Energy demand grew at 1.3% annually for a decade; last year it doubled to 2.4%.

The drivers are obvious once named: AI data centers, EV charging, electrified port fleets, industrial 3D printing. The Port of Long Beach alone is projecting a 6x increase in electricity demand over five years as it electrifies ships, trains, and drayage vehicles. A single AI search requires 10x the power of a traditional web query.

"Energy, energy, energy. The demand is growing by more than twice what it has been historically — and we need all kinds of new energy."
J John Keisler · CEO, Sunstone

Beyond energy, Martin Klecker emphasized that each federal agency publishes Notices of Special Interest — specific research priorities they're actively funding. NIH might want immunology breakthroughs. The USDA wants AgTech pest detection. The EPA wants soil remediation. Reading these notices is the first step to targeted, fundable proposals — not broad grant scattershot.

SBIR / STTR: The Non-Dilutive Engine

Martin Klecker is the panel's practitioner on SBIR (Small Business Innovation Research) and STTR (Small Business Technology Transfer). His core message: these programs were designed precisely for the moment when equity investors won't take the risk — and they were expanded during the 2008 recession when angel and VC dried up.

The programs span 11-12 federal agencies and total approximately $4.3–4.5 billion annually. They're open not just to startups, but to any established business with a disruptive innovation seeking seed funding. The government becomes a non-equity, non-dilutive investor — you keep the company, you keep the IP.

"If you have a disruptive or discontinuous innovation where the equity investor community isn't ready — this is the best way to go. Time-consuming, but worth its weight in gold."
M Martin Klecker · Director, EPIC SBDC

Success rates: historically 11–18% for Phase 1 awards. With Klecker's "extreme handholding" process at UC Riverside, his team gets clients to 35%. The Phase 1 → Phase 2 transition rate has dropped from ~45% to ~30-35%. Key tactic: build a corporate partner relationship before submitting — it signals to reviewers that you have a validated market and a committed commercialization path.

Burton noted the appropriations wildcard: congressional earmarks ("congressionally directed spending") are back. Talk to your member of Congress. Timeline is 1-3 years, but politicians want to bring jobs to their districts — align your pitch to that.

Beyond Grants: Loans, SBIC, and SSBCI

Keisler laid out the government's full capital stack. Grants are first — non-dilutive R&D for the earliest stage. As sectors mature, the government transitions from grants to loan guarantees and credit enhancements, and eventually exits when private lenders see enough certainty to underwrite independently. It's a deliberately staged exit strategy.

Two underutilized programs stood out. First: SBIC funds (Small Business Investment Companies) — private equity and credit funds that get 1:1 or even 2:1 leverage from the SBA. They're required by charter to invest in small businesses at the growth stage. Most entrepreneurs have never heard of them.

"SBIC funds are one of the best kept secrets in government-backed capital. They have to find your companies — they need to meet their charter."
J John Keisler · CEO, Sunstone

Second: SSBCI (State Small Business Credit Initiative). California received $1.2 billion from the Inflation Reduction Act. State Treasurer Fiona Ma has it in the bank and is trying to deploy it. Structure: $800M sits as a loan loss reserve, providing an 80% guarantee against losses to private lenders who make loans to small businesses. The money isn't going anywhere — it's sitting waiting for lenders to use it.

Energy, California & the Moore's Law Moment

For founders worried that federal EV and renewable support is being rolled back under the current administration, Keisler had a direct answer: you live in California. The state is doubling down with a nearly $50 billion commitment to clean and renewable energy through CEC, CARB, and AQMD — specifically targeting startups.

California voters passed a $10 billion climate bond in November 2024. It covers energy production, storage, distribution, virtual power plants, and microgrids — from offshore wind at scale down to AI software managing grid infrastructure. Sunstone Advisors is raising a $250 million clean energy infrastructure fund in partnership with Aries Management.

"This is the Moore's Law moment for renewable energy. We need so much more, so much more quickly — and the technology is already here."
J John Keisler · CEO, Sunstone

A concrete example from the portfolio: a startup converting medium-duty delivery vehicles (18 million in North America) to full battery electric in two days — extending vehicle life 25 years and lowering operating costs. They need charging infrastructure and on-site energy generation. California has programs specifically designed to fund that stack.

IP Rights, March-In, and the Founder's Playbook

The room's concern about government ownership was addressed directly by Klecker. When you win an SBIR/STTR grant and generate IP using federal funding, the government gets a limited, non-exclusive license for internal use only. They are not commercializing it. You own the technology. The Bayh-Dole Act governs this — and its "march-in rights" clause (the government's ability to reassign IP if you fail to commercialize) has essentially never been exercised.
